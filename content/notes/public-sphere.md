# Why Sphere is called Sphere

Sphere is named after the public sphere: the shared space where information becomes visible, debatable, citable, contestable, and accountable.

## Habermas: publicness as infrastructure

Habermas gives Sphere its civic intuition. Public knowledge is not only content that exists somewhere. It becomes public when people can encounter it, question it, cite it, challenge it, and use it to form judgment.

AI agents are becoming intermediaries in that process. They read, summarize, retrieve, rank, and recommend. If they mediate access to knowledge, then public knowledge needs machine-readable conditions: provenance, rights, citation, access policy, and accountability.

## Benjamin: fragments and constellations

Benjamin gives Sphere its form. Knowledge does not always arrive as a closed system. It often appears as fragments: quotations, notes, images, traces, documents, claims, data points, and citations.

A fragment is not weak because it is partial. It becomes meaningful through relation. Fragments can be arranged, juxtaposed, cited, and read as constellations. This is why Sphere treats a fragment as an atomic unit, but never as an isolated one.

## Technical translation

In Sphere, the theory becomes infrastructure. A fragment is a bounded unit of agent-readable knowledge. It can include Markdown content, source files, datasets, media, license metadata, access policy, provenance, and semantic relations to other fragments.

Relations make fragments public in a stronger sense. A fragment can cite, support, extend, update, contradict, derive from, or belong to a constellation. The goal is not simply to expose text to machines, but to preserve the conditions under which knowledge can be trusted, questioned, reused, and attributed.

## Why now

The web was built for human readers, then crawled by machines. The next public sphere will be partly mediated by agents. Sphere starts from a modest question: what should a piece of public knowledge look like when the first reader may be an AI system?

The answer is not a paywall or a black box. It is a fragment with explicit structure, rights, provenance, relations, and signals that help publishers understand whether their knowledge is ready to be read by agents.
